What Is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyberattack where criminals disguise themselves as trusted sources — a bank, a boss, a delivery company — to steal your passwords, financial data, or personal information.

💡 The name "phishing" is like fishing — attackers cast bait hoping someone bites. The "ph" spelling comes from early hacker culture.

6 Types of Phishing Attacks

📧
Email Phishing
The most common type. Fake emails from brands like PayPal or Amazon asking you to "verify" your account by clicking a link.
🎯
Spear Phishing
Targeted attacks using your name, job title, or personal details to appear convincing. Much harder to detect than generic emails.
📱
Smishing (SMS)
Phishing via text messages — fake delivery alerts, bank warnings, or prize notifications containing malicious links.
📞
Vishing (Voice)
Attackers call you pretending to be tech support, your bank, or a government agency demanding immediate action.
🐳
Whaling
Attacks targeting CEOs and executives — the "big fish" whose access and authority is extremely valuable to attackers.
🌐
Clone Phishing
A real email you received is copied and re-sent with a malicious link swapped in for the original legitimate one.

How to Recognize Phishing

Real-world examples of phishing emails and fake websites — with every red flag annotated so you know exactly what to look for.

⚠ Example: Fake PayPal Email

Inbox — Mail
From:security-noreply@paypa1.com⚠ FAKE — "paypa1" not "paypal"
To:you@email.com
Subject:URGENT: Your account will be suspended in 24 hours!⚠ URGENCY TACTIC
Dear Valued Customer⚠ GENERIC — banks know your name,

We have detected suspicious activity on your account. It will be permanently suspended unless you verify your identity immediately.

Click below to secure your account:

➡ http://paypa1-secure-login.xyz/verify⚠ FAKE URL — not paypal.com

This link expires in 24 hours.

— The PayPal Security Team

⚠ Example: Fake Login Page

🔴
⚠ http://paypa1-secure-login.xyz/verify⚠ No HTTPS — not secure
PayPal⚠ Slightly wrong font & colour

Log in to verify your account and prevent suspension.

Clicking "Log In" sends your credentials directly to the attacker⚠ THIS IS THE TRAP

🚩 Red Flags Checklist

⚠
Misspelled domain — paypa1.com vs paypal.com. Always read the sender address character by character.
⚠
Urgency and threats — "suspended in 24 hours", "act now". Legitimate companies do not threaten you by email.
⚠
Generic greeting — "Dear Customer" instead of your real name. Your actual bank knows your name.
⚠
Suspicious links — hover over any link before clicking. The real URL shows at the bottom of your browser.
⚠
No HTTPS padlock — legitimate login pages always use HTTPS. No padlock means the connection is not encrypted.
⚠
Requests for credentials via email — no legitimate company will ever ask for your password by email.
⚠
Unexpected contact — you did not request a reset or review? That alone is a major red flag.

Social Engineering Tactics

Phishing does not break technology — it breaks people. Attackers exploit predictable human psychology. Here are the six core tactics they use.

Urgency
False Time Pressure
Creating panic so you act before you think. When people feel rushed, they skip verification steps entirely.
"Your account will be closed in 2 hours unless you act NOW!"
Authority
Impersonation
Pretending to be someone with power — your CEO, your bank, or the IRS. People comply with authority without questioning.
"This is your CEO. Transfer $5,000 immediately — I will explain later."
Fear
Threat of Loss
Threatening consequences like legal action or account closure to override rational thinking with fear.
"Your tax return shows fraud. Failure to respond will result in arrest."
Curiosity
Enticing Bait
Using prizes, refunds, or shocking news to make you click before thinking. We want prizes to be real.
"You have an unclaimed refund of $847. Claim it within 48 hours."
Trust
Familiarity Exploitation
Using your name, employer, or social media activity to seem like someone you already know and trust.
"Hi [Your Name], following up on our LinkedIn connection — review this contract."
Reciprocity
Fake Helpfulness
Offering help first to build goodwill, then asking for your credentials to "confirm" the fix they made.
"We secured your account. Please verify it was you by logging in here."
💡 Key insight: These tactics do not require hacking your computer — they hack your mind. The best defense is pausing and asking: "Did I initiate this? Can I verify this independently?"

Best Practices & Tips

Practical habits that protect you from phishing — at work and in your personal life.

✅ DO THIS
🔍Verify the sender's full email address — not just the display name
🖱️Hover over links before clicking to preview the real destination URL
🔐Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on all important accounts
📞Call the company directly using a number from their official website — not the email
🛡️Keep your browser, OS, and antivirus updated at all times
📢Report suspicious emails to your IT or security team immediately
🔑Use a password manager — unique passwords for every account
❌ DON'T DO THIS
🚫Click links in unexpected or unsolicited emails — even from known contacts
🚫Download attachments you were not expecting, especially .exe, .zip, .doc files
🚫Share your password, PIN, or OTP with anyone — including "support staff"
🚫Log into sensitive accounts on public Wi-Fi without a VPN
🚫Ignore browser security warnings about unsafe websites
🚫Reuse the same password across multiple accounts
🚫Act on urgent requests without independently verifying them first

🚨 If You Think You Have Been Phished

1️⃣
Do Not Panic
Stay calm. Disconnect from the internet if you downloaded something suspicious.
2️⃣
Change Passwords
Immediately change passwords on the affected account and any account sharing that password.
3️⃣
Report It
Tell your IT team, bank, or relevant authority. Reporting helps protect others too.
